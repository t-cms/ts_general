How Conservative Isolation in Maryland Fueled January 6th

By: Tiasia Saunders

Four years after the January 6th insurrection, President Donald Trump pledged to pardon more than a thousand participants, among them, dozens from Maryland. Of the 46 Marylanders charged in connection with the attack, 32 came from counties that voted overwhelmingly for Joe Biden. This trend of a higher number of January 6th participants coming from blue counties is understandable, as Maryland's blue counties tend to have larger populations. With more people living in these areas, it's statistically likely that a greater number of individuals from these regions would be involved in the insurrection, regardless of their political affiliation.

"It is not difficult to come from a blue county in Maryland, but I think it might have made right-wingers feel angry. Being surrounded by not just Democrats for many cases, but liberal Democrats, and places like Montgomery, Howard, and Baltimore counties," Matthew Crenson, a professor emeritus of political science at Johns Hopkins University, explained.

"He added, 'So they may have, because they were exposed to unfriendly, hostile, political opinions; been induced to become hostile themselves, and that may have driven them to travel to Washington on January 6th.'"

This sense of political isolation is not unique to Maryland but is part of a larger, national trend where individuals in politically homogeneous environments can become more entrenched in their beliefs, sometimes to the point of radicalization.

According to the Becker Friedman Institute report, this isolation creates an aggressive mentality, where individuals who feel marginalized by the dominant political culture around them may seek out violent ways to express their frustrations.

Additionally, this sense of alienation is not confined to rural or conservative areas; in fact, it can thrive even in more liberal environments.

"Yeah. I think, unfortunately, that has been the case, right? Because of, especially because of the internet, like, it's not just that we see, unfortunately, Neo-Nazis in like Southern, deep red states, like, you know, there was, like, a neo-Nazi group that wanted to make a white ethno-state in, like, Maine, right? Like they exist in very blue places too," Jon Lewis, a Research Fellow at the Program on Extremism at George Washington University, said.

In Maryland, particularly in the state's urban and suburban areas, where liberal policies often dominate, some right-leaning individuals might have felt alienated or even demonized by the progressive views of their neighbors. This sense of alienation could have fueled a desire to make a political statement, culminating in the violent actions on January 6th.

Take Frederick County, for example, traditionally a swing county that has seen a growing divide, with more residents leaning towards right-wing ideologies. The county's unique political landscape reflects Maryland's broader national tensions.

Though Frederick County supported Biden in 2020, the county exhibited a notable shift in local politics, with increasing numbers of right-leaning voters. This shift, paired with its proximity to both liberal cities like Baltimore and Washington, D.C. and more conservative rural areas, created an environment ripe for political friction.

As Matthew Crenson explained, the political landscape in Maryland has shifted significantly over time. "The Democrats outnumbered Republicans in the state by two to one. So, the Republicans were confined to the Eastern Shore and Western Maryland, and that was changing too, because some previously Republican areas were becoming more Democratic, like Frederick, as Washington commuters moved west."

In April, the Frederick Gun and Knife Show's gathering for pro-gun Marylanders from various counties served as another reflection of the state's political divide. Under the glow of fluorescent lights, rows of tables displayed weapons, military memorabilia, and graphic t-shirts emblazoned with slogans like "Ammosexual" and "Trump: Better Coverage Than 5G Can You Hear Us Now?"

An attendee, Bruce Byrd, a senior resident of Prince George's County, expressed several concerns about the state of politics in Maryland. "We're running the old timers out, because we're going to turn it into a bedroom community," Byrd said of areas like Frederick County, where many residents commute to work in Washington, D.C.

Further exacerbating this sense of alienation is the rise of radicalization via social media. A 2023 George Washington study described the current generation of extremism as being more intense and driven by encrypted social media platforms where individuals prioritize personal privacy and group secrecy.

Dr. Kyle Larson, a Senior Research Associate at Chicago Project on Security and Threats at the University of Chicago explained that political violence is often shaped by an individual's mindset, online activity, and personal observations.

"Any given instance of political violence is going to be informed in large part or at least in part by what's going on in the head of the individual," Larson said. "The head of the individual may be informed by things like what websites they're spending time reading, but it may also be informed by their personal observation of the world around them."

"You can't fully discount local factors, especially when you're looking at something as individualized as an act of political violence," he added.
